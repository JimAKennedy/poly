If there is a single tradition that Poly was designed to model, it is the polymetric drumming of West and Central Africa. The principle of layering independent rhythmic cycles — each with its own length, density, and social function — did not arrive in Western music theory until the twentieth century. In the Ewe communities of southern Ghana, it has been the organising logic of ensemble music for centuries.<sup>[6](/appendix-references/#ref-6)</sup>

## The Gankogui Bell: E(7,12)

Every Ewe drum ensemble begins with the gankogui, the double iron bell. The bell player establishes a fixed timeline pattern that does not vary. Other drummers orient their parts relative to it, entering at different points, filling different rhythmic spaces. The bell does not lead in a melodic sense — it defines the temporal grid against which everything else is measured.

The standard gankogui pattern distributes seven strokes across twelve pulses. It is Euclidean rhythm E(7,12), and by Toussaint's analysis it is the single most important timeline pattern in sub-Saharan music.<sup>[4](/appendix-references/#ref-4)</sup>

<EuclideanDiagram steps={12} hits={7} />

Notice the asymmetry. The seven hits do not divide twelve evenly — they create an uneven grouping of long and short intervals (2-2-1-2-2-1-2). This asymmetry is not an accident. It is what gives the pattern its characteristic tension, the sense that it is always pulling forward toward the next downbeat without ever quite resolving.

## Role Separation: Timeline, Support, Lead

West African ensembles organise their parts into functional layers. Understanding these roles is the key to building convincing polymetric patches in Poly.

The **timeline** (bell) is fixed and metronomic. It never changes. In Poly, this is your anchor lane — high velocity, no mutation, no phrase gating. To lock a specific bell pattern into place so the engine cannot modulate it, use timeline mode; see [Chapter 18 (Editors and Advanced Views)](/18-editors-and-views/#timeline-mode-and-the-step-editor) for how to enter timeline mode and draw the pattern step by step.

The **support** drums play shorter, sparser patterns that interlock with the bell. In Ewe drumming, the kidi and sogo fill the spaces the bell leaves open. These parts respond to the timeline but remain relatively stable. In Poly, these lanes use fewer hits and lower velocity, with modest mutation values if you want subtle variation.

The **lead** drum (atsimevu in Ewe tradition) improvises. It plays across and against the other parts, creating rhythmic tension and release. In Poly, you model this with higher mutation percentages, phrase gating, and probability — the lead lane should breathe and vary while the timeline holds steady beneath it.

## Polymetric Stacking

The power of the Ewe approach is that support drums often run at different cycle lengths from the bell. A 12-step bell layered with a 7-step support pattern creates a composite rhythm that does not repeat for 84 pulses. Add a 5-step third voice and the full cycle extends to 420 pulses — over thirty bars at 1/8 subdivision. The groove is always familiar but never quite the same.

## Manding Traditions: Djembe Ensembles

West of the Ewe heartland, the Manding traditions of Mali, Guinea, and Senegal build their ensembles around the djembe. Where Ewe drumming stacks many independent cycles, Manding patterns typically lock to a shared cycle length but vary in density and timbre. The dunun bass drums provide a sparse timeline, the sangban plays a mid-density response, and the djembe soloist improvises over both.

You can model this in Poly by keeping all lanes at the same step count but varying hits and velocity. The effect is closer to hocketing — rhythmic interlocking within a single metric frame.

## Building Improvisatory Feel

Traditional lead drumming is never fully random — it follows shared vocabulary and responds to dancers and other musicians. In Poly, you approximate this not by cranking mutation to 100%, but by combining moderate mutation (15-30%) with phrase gating. Set a phrase length of 4-8 beats with a gap of 2-4 beats on the lead lane, and the pattern will breathe — playing for a phrase, resting, then re-entering with variation. The timeline and support lanes, ungated, continue without interruption.

This call-and-response structure is fundamental to West African music. The bell calls. The support responds. The lead converses with both. In Poly, that conversation emerges from the interaction of independent Euclidean cycles, and no two bars are quite the same.
